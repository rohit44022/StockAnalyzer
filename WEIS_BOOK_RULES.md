### Every actionable rule, principle, and framework from the actual book

## I. THE 7 CORE PRINCIPLES (Introduction)

Weis explicitly lists these as the framework for the entire book:

> "In the studies that appear throughout this book, we will:
> 1. Compare effort of the buying or selling with the reward (i.e., volume versus upward or downward progress).
> 2. Watch for ease of movement or lack of movement (i.e., wide price bars versus narrow price bars).
> 3. Consider the meaning of the close within the range of a price bar.
> 4. Watch for shortening of upward or downward thrust.
> 5. Watch for follow-through or lack of follow-through after penetrations of support/resistance (this includes the notion of springs and upthrusts).
> 6. Watch for tests of high-volume or "vertical" areas where price accelerated upward or downward.
> 7. Consider the interaction of price with trend lines, channels, and support/resistance lines, which often highlight the price/volume story."

**Actionable Rule:** Every chart evaluation must check all 7 principles. They are not independent — they work together to tell a story.

---

## II. WHERE TO FIND TRADES (Chapter 1)

### Rule W1: Trades live at the edges of trading ranges
> Weis uses a fishing analogy: "Fish don't swim against the edges. They swim along the edges." Trades are found around the boundaries of trading ranges, not in the middle.

### Rule W2: Action signals at range edges
The key turning points at range boundaries are:
- **Springs** (penetration below support that fails)
- **Upthrusts** (penetration above resistance that fails)
- **Absorption** (overhead selling overcome by buyers, or vice versa)
- **Tests of breakouts and breakdowns**

### Rule W3: Follow-through is the deciding factor
> "Follow-through or lack of follow-through is the deciding factor."
A breakout/breakdown means NOTHING without follow-through. The absence of follow-through after a penetration is one of the most powerful signals.

### Rule W4: Trading ranges have no set patterns
> Concerned with "dynamics not geometry." Do NOT look for cookie-cutter patterns. Read the behavior, not the shape.

---

## III. DRAWING LINES (Chapter 2)

### Rule L1: Support and resistance frame trading ranges
Draw horizontal lines across significant highs and lows to create the framework. These reveal failed attempts to move beyond boundaries.

### Rule L2: Axis lines
Levels that **alternate** between acting as support and resistance. When a stock breaks below a support level, that level often becomes resistance on subsequent rallies (and vice versa).

### Rule L3: Trend lines
- **Demand line** (uptrend): drawn across successive lows
- **Supply line** (downtrend): drawn across successive highs

### Rule L4: Trend channels
- **Normal up-channel**: demand line + parallel line across intervening high (supply line)
- **Normal down-channel**: supply line + parallel line across intervening low (demand line)

### Rule L5: Overbought/oversold via channels
- Overbought = rally above the supply line of an up-channel
- Oversold = decline below the demand line of a down-channel

### Rule L6: Reverse trend lines — THE most predictive line break
> "A move above or below a reverse trend line often will mark the end of a swing."
> "No other trend line break has such predictive value."

- **Reverse uptrend line**: drawn across rising highs in an uptrend
- **Reverse downtrend line**: drawn across falling lows in a downtrend

**Actionable Rule:** A break of a reverse trend line is a stronger signal of trend exhaustion than any other line break.

### Rule L7: Rising wedge = ending action
Converging trend lines (demand line and supply line meeting) indicate the move is ending.

## IV. STORY OF THE LINES (Chapter 3)

### Rule S1: Ice line
Support line drawn across the bottom of a trading range. After a breakdown below the ice line, watch the character of the subsequent rally:
- If rally cannot reach the top of the range → bearish
- Rally back to the ice line that fails = test of breakdown = shorting opportunity

### Rule S2: Confluence of lines produces turning points
When multiple support/resistance lines, trend lines, and channels converge at a price level, that level has heightened importance. The more lines at a point, the greater the significance.

### Rule S3: Apexes (price tightness/convergence)
When price tightens into an apex (narrowing range), the breakout is significant but the DIRECTION cannot be predicted from the apex alone. Key facts:
- Apex on long-term charts = greatest reward
- "No predictive value" for direction — only that a move is imminent

### Rule S4: Dullness interpretation (Wyckoff quote)
> "When a dull market shows its inability to hold rallies... it is technically weak. On the other hand, when there is a gradual hardening in prices; when bear raids fail to dislodge considerable quantities of stock... we may look for an advancing market."

**Actionable Rule:** Dullness near the LOW of a range = bearish. Dullness near the HIGH (with inability to decline) = bullish.

### Rule S5: Gradual hardening > effervescent bubbles
> "Slow rallies more significant than effervescent bubbles."

Steady, gradual price improvement into resistance is more bullish than a sharp, vertical spike.

### Rule S6: Terminal shakeout
A breakdown at the end of a prolonged trading range that resolves a multiyear (or multi-month) base. These produce the largest moves.

## V. LOGIC OF READING BAR CHARTS (Chapter 4)

### Rule B1: Sequential evaluation
Compare each bar to the bars immediately preceding it. Make deductions bar by bar. Don't jump ahead — let the story unfold.

### Rule B2: Three basic elements of every bar
1. **Price range** (high minus low) — wide = ease of movement, narrow = tightness
2. **Position of close** within the range — close near high = buyers in control, close near low = sellers in control
3. **Volume** — effort applied during that bar

### Rule B3: Effort vs. reward (the master principle)
Volume is the EFFORT. Price movement is the REWARD.
- High volume + wide range in trend direction = effort producing reward (healthy trend)
- High volume + narrow range = effort WITHOUT reward (resistance met, reversal likely)
- High volume + close opposite to trend direction = effort being absorbed
- Low volume pullback after high-volume move = lack of opposition to trend

### Rule B4: Volume formulas are "too simplistic"
> Rising price + rising volume = bullish, etc. — Weis explicitly says these are oversimplified.

Volume must be interpreted IN CONTEXT with price range, close position, and what came before. No formula works in isolation.

### Rule B5: Narrow ranges as analytical tool
Narrow ranges indicate:
- Rest after a move (contraction before expansion)
- Lack of interest/participation
- Absorption in progress
- Potential springboard for next move

Specific patterns (from Crabel):
- **NR4**: Narrowest range of last 4 bars
- **NR7**: Narrowest range of last 7 bars
- **2-Bar NR**: 2-bar range narrower than prior session's range
- **3-Bar NR**: 3-bar range narrower than any prior bar
- **ID/NR4**: Inside day that is also NR4 (most powerful)

### Rule B6: Hinge (Wyckoff term)
Price tightness = "precursor to a price swing." When the range contracts significantly after a move, expect a new swing to develop.

### Rule B7: Principle of Contraction/Expansion
Markets alternate between periods of activity (expansion — wide ranges, high volume) and rest (contraction — narrow ranges, low volume). The transition from contraction TO expansion is where trades are born.

### Rule B8: Shortening of Thrust (SOT)
Loss of momentum on successive waves/impulses. Measured by comparing the GAIN from each successive high (in uptrend) or LOSS from each successive low (in downtrend).

> "Uptrends end when the selling waves begin to increase in time and distance or the buying waves shorten."

**Actionable Rule:** If each successive push makes less new ground than the previous one, the trend is exhausting.

### Rule B9: Changes in behavior
The single most important concept for identifying turning points:
- Largest down-bar in X periods after an uptrend = bearish change
- Largest volume on a down-wave after uptrend = supply entering
- Outside downward reversal after new high = bearish change
- Outside upward reversal after new low = bullish change
- First time a selling wave exceeds the prior selling wave in size/volume = bearish change

**Actionable Rule:** Any "first" or "largest" event in the opposite direction to the trend is a change in behavior that signals potential reversal.

## VI. SPRINGS (Chapter 5)

### Rule SP1: Spring definition
> "A washout (penetration) of a trading range or support level that fails to follow through and leads to an upward reversal."

### Rule SP2: Three critical spring concepts
1. **Potential spring position**: identified by LACK OF FOLLOW-THROUGH after a breakdown below support
2. **Springs in uptrend have higher success rate** than springs in downtrend
3. **Volatility and preparation** determine the magnitude of the response

### Rule SP3: Spring size — the boxing analogy
> "If a boxer knocks his opponent out of the ring and into the fifth row of seats, the opponent should not recover."

If the penetration below support is TOO large, it's not a spring — it's a genuine breakdown. Springs are "relatively small penetrations." No precise measurement exists.

### Rule SP4: Volume interpretation on springs
- **Low-volume spring**: washout on LOW volume → selling pressure exhausted, supply spent → bullish
- **High-volume spring**: big effort pushing below support with LITTLE reward (small penetration) → someone absorbing supply → bullish but needs secondary test
- **Heavy volume spring** → higher propensity for secondary test before resumption
- **Low volume spring** → tested less often, may move directly

### Rule SP5: Secondary test of spring
The ideal secondary test shows:
- Narrow range
- Tapering/diminishing volume
- Higher support level than the spring low (holds above the spring)

**Actionable Rule:** After a spring, wait for and evaluate the secondary test. If the test holds on lower volume at a higher level, the spring is confirmed.

### Rule SP6: Gap-up spring
Stock gaps HIGHER the day after breaking below support (in an uptrend). This is powerful in uptrends, rarely works in downtrends.

### Rule SP7: Terminal shakeout
Occurs at the end of a prolonged trading range. The breakdown below the range resolves a major base and leads to the largest moves.

### Rule SP8: Failed springs (CRITICAL WARNING)
> "Failed springs in a downtrend are the bottom picker's nightmare."

In a downtrend, springs frequently fail. The trend context is paramount. Do NOT automatically buy every spring-like pattern.

### Rule SP9: SOT context for springs
If the downward thrust has been SHORTENING as prices approach support, a spring is MORE likely to succeed. The diminishing selling momentum adds context.

### Rule SP10: Springboard
After a spring, if price TIGHTENS (narrow ranges, low volume, consolidates), the stock is on the "springboard" — ready for a larger move. This is an ideal entry point.

### Rule SP11: Spring on a pullback to test a breakout (FAVORITE SETUP)
> Weis explicitly names this as "one of my favorite setups."

Sequence:
1. High-volume breakout above resistance
2. Price corrects back into the area where the breakout occurred (vertical breakout area)
3. New support line forms
4. Spring of this new support line
5. If prices then tighten → buy

### Rule SP12: "I know of no better trading strategy than the spring"
Weis considers the spring the single best trading setup in all of technical analysis.

## VII. UPTHRUSTS (Chapter 6)

### Rule UT1: Upthrust definition
A penetration (breakout) above resistance that FAILS to follow through and reverses downward.

### Rule UT2: Trend context is paramount
> Upthrusts in an uptrend rarely pan out. In a downtrend, upthrusts above a previous correction high have greater probability.

**Actionable Rule:** Only trade upthrusts SHORT when the broader trend is down or the market is in distribution. Avoid shorting upthrusts in strong uptrends.

### Rule UT3: It's not just the breakout bar
> "It's often the cumulative behavior on the preceding price bars and especially the succeeding price bars that tell the story."

Evaluate the bars BEFORE and AFTER the supposed upthrust, not just the breakout bar itself.

### Rule UT4: Narrow-range breakout = suspicious
> "These narrow-range breakouts should always be eyed suspiciously."

A breakout on a narrow range bar does NOT speak of aggressive demand capable of sustaining an uptrend.

### Rule UT5: No precise size for upthrust
> "A new high by 10 to 15 percent seems a reasonable limitation for an upthrust."

If a stock exceeds the previous high by more than ~15%, it's likely a genuine breakout, not an upthrust. A fractional new high on a low-priced stock CAN be an upthrust.

### Rule UT6: Timeframe matters
- **Weekly/monthly upthrusts** → lead to larger downtrends
- **Daily upthrusts** at tops of rallies → may only produce corrections
- **Intraday upthrusts** → frequent, low-risk edge opportunities

### Rule UT7: Multiple upthrusts before markdown
More than one upthrust can occur in a top formation before the markdown begins.

### Rule UT8: Upthrust ≠ permanent high
> "An upthrust represents ending action but not necessarily terminal action."

Just because an upthrust occurred doesn't mean THE ultimate top is in. Markets can recover from upthrusts.

### Rule UT9: Intraday upthrust signals
On intraday charts, a single bar that penetrates a resistance line and reverses downward can be an upthrust. Key confirmation:
- Lack of follow-through after the penetration
- Low volume leading up to the upthrust = not aggressive buying (looks like "stop hunt")
- Secondary test that holds at lower volume

## VIII. ABSORPTION (Chapter 7)

### Rule AB1: Absorption definition
The process through which overhead selling (long liquidation, profit taking, new short selling) is overcome by persistent buying.

### Rule AB2: Six clues for successful absorption (at highs)
1. **Rising supports** within the trading area
2. **Volume increases** around the top of the absorption area
3. **Lack of downward follow-through** after a threatening price bar
4. At the right-hand side, prices **press against the resistance line** without giving ground
5. Sometimes resolved by a **spring**
6. **Minor upthrusts fail** to produce a breakdown

### Rule AB3: Absorption areas are generally shallow
They often form where prices recently accelerated higher and/or where volume rose sharply. Think of them as the pause that refreshes a trend.

### Rule AB4: Absorption by sellers (at lows)
The mirror image — sellers absorbing buying at the bottom of a range:
- **Repeated inability to rally** away from the danger point
- **Hugging the low** → leads to breakdown
- **Persistently heavy volume** against the low → break imminent
- Failed springs → rally attempts look promising but fail or are short-lived

### Rule AB5: Bag-holding
When persistent selling against a low FAILS to produce further weakness, Wyckoff called this "bag-holding." The large operators are trapping shorts.

**Actionable Rule:** If heavy selling against a low produces no new downward progress, the shorts may be trapped.

### Rule AB6: Levitation (climbing wall of worry)
Absorption can manifest as a slow, steady price rise rather than lateral movement. Volatility stays LOW until a high-volume, wide-range bar temporarily stops the move.

### Rule AB7: "Fist" pattern
Tight absorption over just a few price bars that looks like a clenched fist. These resolve in the direction of the preceding trend.

### Rule AB8: Absorption vs. upthrust distinction
> "Distinguishing between an upthrust and absorption sometimes requires paying attention to subtle clues."
> "Chart reading is an art rather than a science built on black and white signals."

Key differences:
- If prices hold above the breakout level and supports rise → absorption (bullish)
- If the heavy volume is on down-bars within the range → potential upthrust/distribution
- If threatening bars fail to produce follow-through → absorption
- If recovery attempts fail → distribution/upthrust

### Rule AB9: Failed springs at lows = sellers absorbing
> "Among these, the failure to respond to threatening price action at highs and failed springs at lows are the best indications of absorption."

When springs at the low of a trading range repeatedly fail, it indicates sellers are absorbing the buying. This is bearish.

## IX. EFFORT VS. REWARD — THE MASTER PRINCIPLE (Throughout)

### Rule ER1: Large effort, small reward = reversal imminent
High volume + narrow range or little new price progress → the effort (volume) is being met by opposing force.

### Rule ER2: Large effort, large reward = trend continuation
High volume + wide range in trend direction → demand (or supply) overcoming opposition.

### Rule ER3: Small effort, large reward = ease of movement
Low volume + wide range in trend direction → no opposition, path of least resistance.

### Rule ER4: Small effort, small reward = dullness
Low volume + narrow range → market at rest, contraction phase. Watch for expansion.

### Rule ER5: Apply effort/reward to waves, not just bars
Cumulative wave volume vs. wave price progress reveals the TRUE force better than per-bar analysis. "The true force of the buying is lost in time."

## X. WAVE ANALYSIS / WEIS WAVE (Chapters 9-10)

### Rule WV1: Price unfolds in waves, not equal time periods
> "A tape reader would know price movement unfolds in waves rather than in equal time periods."

### Rule WV2: Three elements of waves
Every wave should be evaluated by:
1. **Length** (price distance traveled)
2. **Volume** (cumulative volume during the wave)
3. **Duration** (time taken)

### Rule WV3: Buying vs. selling wave comparison
- When buying waves DIMINISH in length, volume, and duration AND selling waves INCREASE → bearish reversal forming
- When selling waves DIMINISH → bullish reversal forming

### Rule WV4: Wave volume reveals true force
> "Volume subdivided into equal chunks of time does interfere with one's ability to discern the true force of the buying and the selling. The message of the volume is lost in time."

Cumulative wave volume provides a MUCH clearer signal than per-bar volume.

### Rule WV5: Climactic wave
When the FINAL wave has the heaviest volume with little new price progress, it is climactic and signals exhaustion.

### Rule WV6: Low-volume correction within a trend
After a large impulse wave, a correction on LOW volume indicates lack of opposing pressure. The trend should resume.

### Rule WV7: Tests of high-volume areas
Corrections often pull back to test the area where prices previously accelerated (high-volume breakout zones). If the test holds on low volume → trend intact.

### Rule WV8: Flat wave with heavy volume
> "A great deal of time and volume without making any upward progress — the personification of weakness."

A nearly flat wave angle with heavy volume is extremely bearish (or bullish if on the sell-side). This indicates absorption by the opposing side.

## XI. SHORTENING OF THRUST — EXPLICIT GUIDELINES (Chapter 10)

Weis provides his ONLY numbered guidelines in the entire book:

### Rule SOT1: After 3-4 successive waves
> "After three or four successive waves or impulses up or down, look for a shortening of the thrust in the final wave."

The wave usually makes little progress and volume declines = tired demand/supply. Sometimes volume stays heavy but shortening of progress = large effort, small reward.

### Rule SOT2: More than 4 waves with persistent SOT
> "When there are more than four successive waves and shortening of the thrust persists, the trend may be too strong to trade against."

**WARNING:** Don't automatically fade a trend just because you see SOT — if it keeps going, the trend is overpowering.

### Rule SOT3: Only 2 waves = consider spring/upthrust instead
> "When there are only two waves with small progress in the second wave, consider a spring or upthrust."

Ideally low volume; heavy volume acceptable if progress is small.

### Rule SOT4: Measure from price bars, not just waves
> "The shortening of the thrust is determined mostly by the price bars' highs and lows rather than by the waves' turning points."

But the wave volume tells the story about strength or weakness.

### Rule SOT5: Always check the larger picture
> "When thrust shortening appears, always consider the larger picture."

If an upthrust just formed at a major level, don't take long on SOT of the decline — the upthrust overrides.

### Rule SOT6: Be selective against the trend
> "When prices are moving above/below turning points established during the previous up-/down-waves and breaking trend lines, be highly selective if trading against the trend."

SOT in the direction of a counter-trend move may be a trap.

## XII. COMMON TRADE SETUPS (Chapter 10)

### Rule TS1: Spring/upthrust + change in behavior + low-volume test
1. Spring or upthrust occurs
2. Bullish/bearish change in behavior follows
3. Selling/buying pressure diminishes on next pullback
4. Take the trade with close stop below/above the extremity

### Rule TS2: High-volume breakout + low-volume test
1. Breakout/breakdown on high volume
2. Watch the character of the pullback
3. Low volume on pullback = successful test, trend should resume
4. Stop below/above the pullback pivot

### Rule TS3: Two basic requirements
> "Patience to wait for the setups to appear, and operating without any bias — don't tell the market what it will do, let it tell you."

## XIII. CHANGES IN BEHAVIOR — Key Signals (Throughout)

### Changes signaling BEARISH turn:
- Largest down-bar in many periods (new "high" for selling)
- Largest volume on a down-wave since the uptrend began
- Outside downward reversal after new high
- First selling wave to exceed previous selling waves
- Wide-open decline after sideways movement
- Rally losing ability to hold gains (strong open, weak close repeating)

### Changes signaling BULLISH turn:
- Largest up-bar in many periods after decline
- Outside upward reversal from new low
- Heavy volume on an up-bar after sustained decline (demand surfacing)
- First buying wave to exceed previous buying waves
- Inability to follow through on selling after threatening action
- Closing near highs on successive days after weakness

## XIV. POINT-AND-FIGURE AND RENKO (Chapter 11)

### Rule PF1: Projections from congestion
Count columns (or boxes) across a congestion area multiplied by the reversal unit. Conservative: count to the nearest "wall." Maximum: count the full span.

### Rule PF2: P&F counts are guidelines, NOT absolutes
> "Point-and-figure counts are purely mechanical and have no magical powers. Sometimes they have pinpoint accuracy, and other times they fail miserably. No trade should be established solely because of point-and-figure counts."

### Rule PF3: Renko time per brick = effort indicator
When a renko brick takes an unusually long time to complete, it signals absorption/resistance at that level. Heavy time + no progress = effort without reward.

### Rule PF4: Time can substitute for volume
On charts where volume is unavailable (forex, yield curves), the duration of each wave/column/brick serves as a proxy for effort.

## XV. META-PRINCIPLES AND PHILOSOPHICAL FRAMEWORK

### Rule M1: Art, not science
> "Chart reading is an art rather than a science built on black and white signals."

Rules cannot be mechanically applied. Context is everything.

### Rule M2: Accumulation/distribution models NOT from Wyckoff
> "Wyckoff never devised such an interpretation of accumulation and distribution. They were added after his death."

The standard Wyckoff phases (accumulation, markup, distribution, markdown) were created by later students, NOT by Wyckoff himself.

### Rule M3: No cookie-cutter patterns
> "These patterns can quickly become cookie prints... They lead to rigid rather than creative thinking."

### Rule M4: Intuitive judgment
> Wyckoff's goal was to help traders "develop an intuitive judgment" — through prolonged study and observation, not rote rules.

### Rule M5: "The market is like a slowly revolving wheel"
> "Whether the wheel will continue to revolve in the same direction, stand still or reverse depends entirely upon the forces which come in contact with its hub and tread."

### Rule M6: Composite operator
Wyckoff's concept of large operators (insiders, pools) whose accumulated activity is visible in price/volume patterns. They accumulate at lows, distribute at highs, and engineer springs/shakeouts to trigger weak holders.

### Rule M7: Anticipate, don't react
> "Money is made by anticipating what is coming — not by waiting till it happens and going with the crowd."

### Rule M8: Finding trades vs. seeking trades
> "I find trades rather than seek them."

Don't force trades. Let the setups come to you. The best trades require no effort — they are obvious once you see the behavior.

## XVII. CRITICAL DIFFERENCES FROM OUR CURRENT IMPLEMENTATION

### What the book says vs. what we built:

1. **No numeric thresholds in the book.** Weis teaches QUALITATIVE reading. Our numeric thresholds (1.5x volume ratios, etc.) are all calibration values we invented.

2. **Wyckoff phases (ACCUMULATION/DISTRIBUTION/MARKUP/MARKDOWN) are NOT from Wyckoff or Weis.** They were "added after his death." Our phase detection system is from the post-Wyckoff course, not this book.

3. **Springs terminology credited to Bob Evans, not Wyckoff.** "Wyckoff did not write about springs per se."

4. **Chart reading explicitly called an art, not a science.** Our algorithmic approach contradicts Weis's core philosophy. He warns against "rigid rather than creative thinking."

5. **Follow-through is the deciding factor.** Our rules evaluate individual signals but often don't check what happens AFTER the signal (follow-through assessment).

6. **Wave analysis (Weis Wave) is the book's unique contribution.** We don't implement cumulative wave volume analysis — arguably the most important tool in the book.

7. **Effort vs. result on WAVES** is more important than on individual bars. Our volume analysis is bar-based, not wave-based.

8. **Reverse trend lines** are called the most predictive line break. We don't detect these.

9. **Context of the larger trend** is always paramount. Weis repeatedly shows how the same pattern succeeds or fails depending on the dominant trend.

10. **The book explicitly warns against rigid pattern recognition.** Our algorithmic rules are exactly what Weis cautions against.
