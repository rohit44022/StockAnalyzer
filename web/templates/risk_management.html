What is Optimal f?
The optimal f is the exact fraction of your account equity you should risk on each trade to maximise long-term geometric growth. It's NOT about maximising the average return — it's about maximising the compound growth rate, which is what actually grows your account.
HPR = 1 + f × (−Trade ÷ |Biggest Loss|) TWR = HPR₁ × HPR₂ × … × HPRₙ   (Terminal Wealth Relative) Geometric Mean = TWR^(1/N)   (needs to be > 1.0 to grow) Warning: Trading at full optimal f produces SEVERE drawdowns (often 30–95%). In practice, use half-f or less and accept slower but smoother growth.

Position Sizing with f
Once you know optimal f, calculating position size is straightforward:
f₹ = |Biggest Loss| ÷ f   (rupees to risk per "unit") Units = Account Equity ÷ f₹ Shares = Units × (f₹ ÷ Price per Share) The f₹ (f-rupee) tells you how much capital to allocate per unit of trading. If your account has ₹1,00,000 and f₹ is ₹50,000, you can trade 2 units. Use the fraction slider above to adjust how aggressively you want to trade.
Why Statistical Tests Matter
Before applying optimal f, you MUST verify two things:
1. Dependency: Are your trade outcomes random, or do they have patterns? If trades are dependent (streaking or alternating), you can exploit this by adjusting position size based on the last trade's outcome.
2. Distribution: Are your trades Normally distributed? If yes, you can use the parametric (formula-based) optimal f. If not, use the empirical (brute-force) method — which is what we default to.

Arc Sine Law
The Arc Sine Law says: in a random series of trades, you're MOST likely to spend almost ALL the time either winning or losing — NOT 50/50. Long drawdowns are statistically NORMAL, not a sign your system is broken.
Understanding Drawdowns
A drawdown is the peak-to-trough decline in your equity. At optimal f, expect drawdowns of 30–95% of peak equity! This is the "price" of maximum growth.
Drawdown % = (Peak − Trough) ÷ Peak × 100 The key question is: can you psychologically tolerate the drawdown? If not, reduce your f fraction. Remember: a 50% drawdown needs a 100% gain to recover!
Historical Volatility
Historical Volatility (HV) measures how wildly a stock's price has been swinging, expressed as an annualised percentage. It's the same volatility used in Black-Scholes option pricing.
HV = σ(ln(Close_i / Close_{i-1})) × √252   (annualised) Higher volatility = bigger daily swings = larger optimal f₹ (you need more capital per position). When volatility spikes, consider reducing your fraction of f to control risk.
Efficient Frontier (Markowitz + Vince)
The Efficient Frontier shows the best possible portfolio combinations. Each point represents a different allocation among your stocks. Points ON the frontier give the maximum return for their level of risk.
Portfolio Return = Σ(w_i × AHPR_i)   (weighted average) Portfolio Risk = √(Σ Σ w_i × w_j × COV(i,j))   (accounts for diversification) Vince's insight: use the Geometric efficient frontier (GHPR), not just the arithmetic one. The geometric optimal is where AHPR − 1 = Variance.
