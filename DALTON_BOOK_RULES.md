### Every actionable rule, principle, and framework from the actual book

## I. CORE FRAMEWORK — Auction Market Theory

### Rule D1: Market = Continuous Two-Way Auction
Markets continuously auction from high to low, and low to high. Price advertises opportunity, time regulates opportunity, volume validates success or failure of advertised opportunity. All three dimensions matter — price alone is price blindness.

### Rule D2: The Two Big Questions (asked EVERY session)
1. **Which way is the market trying to go?** (attempted direction)
2. **Is it doing a good job?** (directional performance)
These two questions drive every single analysis from day timeframe to long-term.

### Rule D3: Two Most Important Concepts
**Excess** and **Balance.** Excess marks the end of one auction and beginning of another. Balance = equilibrium between buyers and sellers. Every trade opportunity comes from excess or a breakout from balance.

### Rule D4: Price Is Just Advertising
Price is a one-dimensional input. What matters is the RESPONSE to price — which timeframe responded, how strong was the volume response, and where (innovators vs laggards) on the diffusion curve are the responders.

## II. MARKET PROFILE BUILDING BLOCKS (Chapter 2)

### Rule D5: TPO (Time Price Opportunity)
Each 30-minute period is assigned a letter. The accumulation of letters at each price level shows where time was spent — this IS the Market Profile.

### Rule D6: Initial Balance (IB)
The first hour's trading range, established primarily by locals/day-timeframe traders. IB width predicts day type:
- **Wide IB** = OTF (Other Timeframe) entered early → Normal day likely → two-sided trade
- **Narrow IB** = Locals only → IB easy to break → potential for Trend or Double-Distribution day
- **Moderate IB** = OTF may enter later → Normal Variation likely

### Rule D7: Range Extension
Activity beyond the IB confirms OTF (Other Timeframe) presence. Without range extension, it's a day-timeframe-only affair with limited opportunity.
- Range extension on ONE side = directional conviction
- Range extension on BOTH sides = Neutral day (two-sided)
- Multiple periods of range extension = stronger conviction
- NO range extension = Nontrend day (stand aside)

### Rule D8: Value Area (VA)
The price region where 70% of the day's TPOs (or volume) occurred — approximately one standard deviation in a normal distribution. This is where two-sided trade was accepted. The VA is the institutional definition of "fair value."

### Rule D9: Point of Control (POC) / Fairest Price
The longest line of TPOs closest to center of the range = the fairest price at which business is being conducted.
- POC migrating higher = OTF buying in control
- POC migrating lower = OTF selling in control
- POC stationary = balance / no conviction
- **Selling below the POC = "short in the hole"** (poor location → short-covering rally likely)
- **Buying above the POC = "long in the hole"** (poor location → long-liquidation break likely)

### Rule D10: Single-Print Tails
≥2 TPOs of single prints at the extremes. Tails represent aggressive OTF rejection.
- **Buying tail** (at low) = aggressive OTF buyer appeared → support
- **Selling tail** (at high) = aggressive OTF seller appeared → resistance
- Longer tail = more conviction
- Last-period tails not technically valid (market ran out of time)
- **NO tail (poor high/low)** = no aggressive OTF participation → auction incomplete → will likely be revisited

### Rule D11: Closing Range
Last half-hour's settlement area. If close is near the extreme of the day's range, sentiment is strongly directional. The close is the last reference for the next session's opening.

## III. SIX DAY TYPES (Chapter 2) — Conviction Scale

Listed from LOWEST to HIGHEST conviction:

### Rule D12: Nontrend Day
- No range extension, no OTF activity, tight range
- Characteristics: Often before major announcements; very low volume
- **Action: STAND ASIDE.** No facilitation = no opportunity.

### Rule D13: Neutral Day
- Range extension on BOTH sides → both OTF buyer and seller present
- Two subtypes:
  - **Neutral-Center**: Close near center → balanced, no winner
  - **Neutral-Extreme**: Close near one extreme → conviction in that direction
- **Neutral-Extreme special situation (Rule D50):** 92% probability next session opens within or better than VA. HIGH PROBABILITY entry.

### Rule D14: Normal Day
- Wide IB from early OTF entry → two-sided trade within wider range
- Expect rotational trading within the IB
- Classic two-timeframe market

### Rule D15: Normal Variation Day
- Moderate IB, then OTF extends range on one side
- More directional than Normal, less than Trend
- Good opportunities on the side of the range extension

### Rule D16: Trend Day
- Open forms one extreme; one-timeframe activity all day
- Elongated thin profile (≤4-5 TPOs wide at any level)
- **MOST COSTLY to be wrong about** — the entire day is directional
- **Action: Get on board with the trend early. Do NOT fade a Trend day.**
- Close near the opposite extreme from the open

### Rule D17: Double-Distribution Trend Day
- Narrow IB early, then aggressive OTF move creates second distribution
- Two clusters of value separated by single prints
- **Second-highest conviction day type** after Trend Day
- The single prints between distributions are powerful support/resistance

## IV. FOUR OPEN TYPES (Chapter 4) — Conviction Ranking

Ranked from HIGHEST to LOWEST conviction:

### Rule D18: Open-Drive
- Market drives away from the open immediately → extreme holds most of day
- Highest conviction for direction of the drive
- **Action: Enter EARLY. Do not wait for structure confirmation.**
- If market returns through the opening price later → conditions have fundamentally changed

### Rule D19: Open-Test-Drive
- Market opens, tests beyond a reference point, finds no business, reverses
- Second most reliable extreme formation
- **Action: Enter after reversal confirmed. Stop beyond the test extreme.**

### Rule D20: Open-Rejection-Reverse
- Lower conviction. Initial extreme holds <50% of the day
- Expect Normal or Normal Variation day
- **KEY: PATIENCE. Wait for rotation back toward value before entering.**

### Rule D21: Open-Auction (In Range / Out of Range)
- **In Range**: No OTF conviction at open → expect Nontrend/Normal/Neutral
  - **Action: Trade value area extremes responsively or stand aside**
- **Out of Range**: Despite nonconvictional opening, market is out of balance
  - Greater chance of directional conviction → potential for big day
  - **Action: Be alert for directional development; don't dismiss**

## V. OPENING vs PREVIOUS DAY (Chapter 4) — Range Estimation

### Rule D22: Open Within Previous Value Area — Acceptance
Market in balance. Expect range ≈ previous day's range.
- **Range Estimation**: Identify the secure extreme → superimpose previous day's range length from that extreme → allow ±10%

### Rule D23: Open Within Previous Value Area — Rejection (Breakout)
Market drives out of value without building double TPOs at the edge.
- Breaking out of balance. **Unlimited range potential.**
- **Action: GO WITH the breakout.**

### Rule D24: Open Outside Value but Within Range — Acceptance
Slightly out of balance. Range similar but shifted to overlap on one side.

### Rule D25: Open Outside Value but Within Range — Rejection
Breaking out. **Unlimited range potential** in the breakout direction.

### Rule D26: Open Outside Range — Acceptance
Market significantly out of balance. Either continues or balances at new level.
- **Trend day likely.** Unlimited range in breakout direction.
- **Action: Trade with the direction of the gap/breakout.**

### Rule D27: Open Outside Range — Rejection
Dynamic move in opposite direction. **Unlimited range potential in rejection direction.**

## VI. AUCTION MECHANICS (Chapter 4)

### Rule D28: One-Timeframe vs Two-Timeframe
- **One-timeframe**: One OTF participant in total control. Each successive half-hour auction extends in one direction. Characteristic of Trend days.
  - One-timeframing UP: each period's LOW does not exceed the prior period's low
  - One-timeframing DOWN: each period's HIGH does not exceed the prior period's high
  - **Action: NEVER trade counter to one-timeframing. It is financially dangerous.**
- **Two-timeframe**: Both buyers and sellers sharing control. Rotational trade. Characteristic of Normal/NV/Neutral days.

### Rule D29: Timeframe Transition Identification
Four categories:
1. No transition (one-TF or two-TF all day)
2. One-TF → Two-TF (probe beyond reference fails; pace slows)
3. Two-TF → One-TF (breakout through reference; pace accelerates)
4. One-TF direction A → One-TF direction B (Neutral day formation)

**Confirmation: Double TPO prints against the one-timeframe direction confirm transition.**
- Time provides the signal (price spending too much time in one place = slowing)
- Structure provides the confirmation (double TPOs)

### Rule D30: Auction Failure
Market auctions beyond a reference point and FAILS to follow through.
- **Result: Swift rejection in the opposite direction.**
- Magnitude of the rejection scales with the significance of the reference point tested.
- **Longer timeframe failures = greater magnitude rejections.**
- Day-timeframe failure → small counter-move. Long-term failure → major reversal.

### Rule D31: Excess
Market auctions too far in one direction. Aggressive OTF enters from the other side to return price to value.
- Forms: **tails** (day TF excess), **single prints** (intermediate), **gaps** (strongest form)
- Excess creates future support/resistance
- **Excess marks the END of one auction and the BEGINNING of a new auction.**

### Rule D32: Rotation Factor
Objective measure of attempted direction. For each period:
- If current high > prior high, assign +1; if lower, assign -1
- If current low > prior low, assign +1; if lower, assign -1
- Cumulative sum across all periods = net Rotation Factor
- Only answers "which way trying to go" — NOT "how good a job"

## VII. PROFILE FORMATIONS (Chapter 4)

### Rule D33: Short-Covering Rally (P-Formation)
- P-shaped profile: activity concentrated in the upper portion
- Follows strong selling (decline + excess low)
- **This is OLD business covering, NOT new buying**
- Successive higher auctions WEAKEN in a short-covering rally
- **Market resumes prior course once covering diminishes**
- **Action: Offers selling opportunity once rally stalls. Do NOT initiate new longs.**

### Rule D34: Long-Liquidation Break (b-Formation)
- b-shaped profile: activity concentrated in the lower portion
- Mirror of short-covering. Old business liquidating longs.
- **Market corrects upward after liquidation ends**
- **Action: Offers buying opportunity once liquidation exhausts itself.**

### Rule D35: Ledges
A half-completed distribution that repeatedly attracts the responsive participant.
- If ledge holds → price moves significantly in the opposite direction
- If ledge breaks → continuation through the ledge

### Rule D36: High-Volume Areas
Price levels where significant volume/TPOs accumulated = acceptance/value.
- **HIGH volume ATTRACTS price** — like a magnet
- Price slows and becomes rotational when re-entering high-volume areas
- Short-term market memory: primarily relevant in the near term

### Rule D37: Low-Volume Areas
Price levels where little trading occurred = rejection/conviction.
- **LOW volume REJECTS price** — acts as barrier
- Should hold as support/resistance
- If pierced ("balloon" analogy) → price moves swiftly through
- Gaps, single prints, tails are all forms of low-volume areas
- **Action: Trade IN FRONT OF low-volume areas (expect swift rejection).** If price penetrates, expect rapid continuation.

## VIII. LONG-TERM TRADING (Chapter 4, Section II)

### Rule D38: Long-Term Attempted Direction Indicators
1. **Auction Rotations**: Compare daily cumulative rotation factors across days
2. **Range Extension**: Persistent daily range extension reveals long-term tendency
3. **Long-Term Excess**: Island days (most extreme), long-term tails, gaps (most powerful)
4. **Buying/Selling Composite Days**: Open in bottom/top quarter of range indicates composite buying/selling

### Rule D39: Long-Term Directional Performance
Three measures to evaluate "how good a job":
1. **Volume**: Greater volume = better trade facilitation. Compare to running average.
2. **Value-Area Placement**: Higher/lower/overlapping/inside/outside vs previous day
3. **Value-Area Width**: Wider VA = greater volume/facilitation; narrow VA = poor facilitation

### Rule D40: The 30 Directional Performance Relationships
Combine attempted direction (up/down) × volume (higher/lower/unchanged) × VA placement (higher/lower/overlapping/unchanged) to produce performance ratings from "very strong" to "very weak."

Key combinations:
- **Attempting UP + Higher Volume + Higher VA** = VERY STRONG (trend healthy)
- **Attempting UP + Lower Volume + Lower VA** = VERY WEAK (trend failing)
- **Attempting DOWN + Higher Volume + Lower VA** = VERY STRONG decline
- **Attempting DOWN + Lower Volume + Higher VA** = VERY WEAK decline

### Rule D41: Trending vs Bracketed Markets
- **Trend** = divergence of price from value; VAs moving in clear direction
- **Bracket** = equilibrium; auction between two reference points
- Markets trend only **20-30% of the time**
- Cycle: **Trend → Bracket → Trend** (never trend up → immediately trend down)
- VA overlap = trend slowing; VAs separating = trend accelerating

### Rule D42: Bracket Rules (4 rules)
1. **Monitor direction and location within bracket.** Trade responsively: buy below bracket VA, sell above.
2. **Markets test bracket extremes 3-5 times on average before breaking out.**
3. **Markets fluctuate within bracket** (top→middle→bottom etc.), not beeline to extreme.
4. **Monitor bracket extremes for acceptance/rejection** before entering.

### Rule D43: Bracket → Trend Transition
Excess confirmed by sustained follow-through. Initiative activity overwhelms responsive activity. Gaps/tails often appear at transition points.

### Rule D44: Trend Trading Rules
- No "good" trade location early in a trend — just get on board and monitor for continuation
- **Later in trend**: trade responsively (buy dips in uptrend, sell rallies in downtrend)
- **NEVER trade against a trend**
- Monitor by comparing volume on with-trend vs against-trend days
- Volume increasing in trend direction = continuation
- Volume decreasing in trend direction = exhaustion

### Rule D45: Trend → Bracket Transition
Responsive participant matches initiator's influence. Excess appears at trend extreme. VAs begin to overlap.

### Rule D46: Long-Term Auction Failures
Same principle as day-timeframe but with **greater magnitude**. Failures at long-term reference points → major reversals. The longer the timeframe of the tested reference, the greater the subsequent rejection.

### Rule D47: Long-Term Short Covering / Long Liquidation
Same P/b patterns over days/weeks. Key = monitor for continuation:
- Poor continuation after initial move = covering/liquidation, NOT new business
- **Corrective action doesn't always mean lower prices.** Can correct while price stays higher (old buyers sell, new buyers enter simultaneously)
- Correction within higher value = exceptionally strong market

## IX. SPECIAL SITUATIONS (Chapter 4)

### Rule D48: 3-to-I Days (HIGHEST PROBABILITY MECHANICAL TRADE)
When Initiative tail + Initiative TPO count + Initiative range extension ALL point the same direction:
- **94% of the time** next day opens within or better than VA
- **97% of the time** next day closes within or better than VA
- **Action: MECHANICAL trade entry. Use VA boundaries for stops.**

### Rule D49: Value-Area Rule
If market opens outside previous VA and is ACCEPTED back into VA (double TPOs), it will likely trade **completely through that VA**.
Probability influenced by:
- Distance from value (closer = higher probability)
- VA width (narrower = easier to traverse)
- Market direction (with-trend = higher probability)
- **Action: Once accepted back into VA, trade for full VA traverse.**

### Rule D50: Neutral-Extreme Days
Neutral day closing on one extreme:
- **92% of the time** next session trades within or better during first 90 minutes
- **Action: Place orders within VA for favorable location.**

### Rule D51: Spikes
Swift breakout from value in the last few periods of the day. Use spike range for next-day analysis:
- **Open within spike** = balance/confirmation of breakout direction
- **Open beyond spike** in same direction = extreme bullish/bearish continuation
- **Open opposite direction** (retreat through spike) = rejection of breakout
- **Spike extremes valid only for first test.** After that, significance diminishes.

### Rule D52: Balance-Area Breakouts
Overlapping value across multiple sessions, then a breakout:
- **"A trade you almost have to do"** — GO WITH the breakout
- Often preceded by a failed auction in the opposite direction (outside day)
- **Risk minimal, profit potential very high.**

### Rule D53: Gaps (Three Types)
1. **Break-away gap**: Start of a new trend. Occurs at bracket extremes or excess points.
2. **Acceleration gap**: Mid-trend. Confirms continuation.
3. **Exhaustion gap**: End of a trend. Often filled quickly.

Key rules:
- Trade with the initiative that caused the gap
- Stops at gap erasure point
- Most gaps filled within first hour IF going to be filled
- Longer a gap holds unfilled = greater continuation probability
- **Gap = form of excess = invisible single prints = strongest form of excess**
- Bigger the gap = smaller the odds it will be filled that day
- Big gap = longer TF traders caught wrong way, will trade in gap direction to adjust inventory

## X. MARKETS TO AVOID (Chapter 4)

### Rule D54: Four Markets to Stay Out Of
1. **Nontrend days** — low volume, no facilitation
2. **Nonconviction days** — appears normal but no OTF reference points; random rotation
3. **Long-term nontrend markets** — erratic, no long-term conviction
4. **News-influenced markets** — volatility too extreme; balance inventory before announcements

### Rule D55: News/Major-Auction-Direction Matrix (8 Scenarios)
| Auction Direction | News | Market Response | Sentiment |
|---|---|---|---|
| Up | Bullish | Up | Expected — no edge |
| Up | Bullish | Down | VERY WEAK |
| Up | Bearish | Up | **VERY STRONG** |
| Up | Bearish | Down | Expected — no edge |
| Down | Bullish | Up | Expected — no edge |
| Down | Bullish | Down | **VERY WEAK** |
| Down | Bearish | Up | **VERY STRONG** |
| Down | Bearish | Down | Expected — no edge |

**Key insight**: When the market moves AGAINST the news, sentiment is at its strongest (or weakest). The non-obvious reaction is the informative one.

## XI. CHAPTER 7 — EXPERIENCE (Updated 2012 Edition)

### Rule D56: Preparation Trumps Everything
Before every session, develop a tactical plan with at least three scenarios:
1. What if the auction moves UP?
2. What if the auction moves DOWN?
3. What if it REMAINS in balance?

Preparation → Mental flexibility → Reduced price blindness. Unprepared traders make impulsive trades driven by emotion.

### Rule D57: Multi-Timeframe Analysis Sequence
Prepare by reviewing timeframes from LONGEST to SHORTEST:
1. Monthly bar → overall trend context
2. Weekly bar → intermediate direction, one-timeframing status
3. Daily bar → recent direction and structural detail
4. Daily Profile → value area, POC, structural cues

This prevents price blindness by establishing context before the session.

### Rule D58: Overnight Inventory
Measure overnight trade relative to prior session settle:
- Majority above settle = **overnight inventory LONG**
- Majority below settle = **overnight inventory SHORT**
- Equal distribution = **neutral**

Key principles:
- Overnight volume ≈ 25% of pit session. Weaker-hands, shorter-timeframe traders.
- If inventory gets too long/short → **expect inventory adjustment shortly after open**
- If overnight inventory is long and market does NOT adjust → **strong market signal**
- **Not being aware of potential adjustment can mislead you for the entire session.**

### Rule D59: Three Balanced-Market Scenarios
When trading a balanced market:
1. **Remain within balance** → rotational, trade extremes responsively
2. **Look above/below balance and FAIL** → breakout failure → trade toward OPPOSITE extreme of balance range
3. **Look above/below balance and ACCELERATE** → breakout completion → go-with trade

**Always prepare for all three.** Mental flexibility beats rigid prediction.

### Rule D60: Timeframe Dominance
- **Day timeframe in control**: Lower volume, less range extension, mechanical references dominate (opening, halfback, prior settle, prior high/low)
- **Other timeframe in control**: Day-timeframe references become irrelevant. Longer-timeframe traders trump shorter-timeframe traders ALWAYS.
- Day traders who fade longer-timeframe activity get "run over"
- **If you gave back 3 weeks of profits in one day → you were fighting the other timeframe**

### Rule D61: Poor Highs / Poor Lows (MOST IMPORTANT OMISSION from 1st Edition)
A lack of buying tail = **poor low** (auction not complete; will be revisited).
A lack of selling tail = **poor high** (auction not complete; will be revisited).

Context behind poor lows/highs:
- Volume below relative average for past several days
- Tempo halting or slow
- Profile more truncated than usual
- **Single poor low = one data point (limited attention)**
- **Multiple successive poor lows = EXPONENTIAL RISK for longs**
- Once inventory adjusts, market often returns to take out the initial high/low to complete the auction — **but not always on the same day** (acting too soon is a common error)

### Rule D62: Too-Elongated Profiles = Reversal
An elongated Market Profile is normally a sign of auction continuation. But when the profile becomes **TOO elongated** (inventory extremely one-sided), the result is often the OPPOSITE — a sharp reversal.
- Extremely short inventory after rapid decline → short-covering rally
- Extremely long inventory after rapid advance → long-liquidation break

### Rule D63: Anomalies = Structural Weakness
A single price or price level that lacks symmetry in the Profile.
- Represents structural weakness
- Often revisited during current or following session
- Combined with poor lows/highs, identifies exponential risk zones

### Rule D64: Destination Trades
Once a directional auction gets underway, it tends to travel to a **visually prominent reference point**:
- Day TF: Previous day's high/low, 3-day high/low, top/bottom of a gap
- Long TF: Bracket high/low, weekly/monthly extremes
- **Destinations don't have to be reached**, but they serve as benchmarks for progress
- Understanding destinations helps sort out conflicting multi-timeframe data

### Rule D65: Cognitive Dissonance as Opportunity
When you hold two opposing thoughts simultaneously (e.g., market is rising but structure is weakening), the dissonance can be positive:
- Recognize that higher prices without confirming structure/volume = better downside opportunity to follow
- **Being prepared for change affords the best trade location and opportunity**
- What DIDN'T happen is often more informative than what did

### Rule D66: We Are All Day Traders
Every trader, regardless of timeframe, is a day trader on entry and exit day.
- **Too eager to enter at open = impulsive trade = rarely works**
- Open within balance relative to previous range → opportunities limited → WAIT for better location
- The closer to center of previous range, the LOWER the urgency to trade early
- The more out of balance (especially gaps), the GREATER the early opportunity

## XII. INITIATIVE vs RESPONSIVE FRAMEWORK (Chapter 3)

### Rule D67: Four Types of Activity (Relative to Previous Day's VA)
1. **Initiative Buying**: Buying within or above previous day's VA → strongest bullish conviction
2. **Initiative Selling**: Selling within or below previous day's VA → strongest bearish conviction
3. **Responsive Buying**: Buying below previous day's VA → reactive, buying "good prices"
4. **Responsive Selling**: Selling above previous day's VA → reactive, selling "good prices"

**Initiative activity has STRONGEST conviction.** Initiative = they came hunting. Responsive = they were lured by price.

### Rule D68: When Responsive Becomes Initiative
If responsive buying below value leads to a sustained rally back through and above value → the responsive activity has "graduated" to initiative. This transition signals real conviction.

## XIII. VOLUME PRINCIPLES (Throughout)

### Rule D69: Volume Validates Everything
Volume is the primary measure of directional performance.
- Greater volume = better trade facilitation = trend continuation more likely
- Lower volume = poor facilitation = trend exhaustion more likely
- Always compare to a running average (relative, not absolute)

### Rule D70: Volume at Price Level (Market Profile Lens)
- Wide profile (many TPOs) at a price = HIGH volume = acceptance = value = price attracted/slowed here
- Thin profile (few TPOs) at a price = LOW volume = rejection = barrier = price moves swiftly through

### Rule D71: Volume Should Always Be Evaluated Relatively
Volume on a given day means nothing in isolation. Compare:
- To the prior day
- To the running 5-10 day average
- With-trend days vs against-trend days
- Structure (elongated = healthy, truncated = unhealthy) determines quality of volume

## XIV. PSYCHOLOGICAL / SELF-UNDERSTANDING (Chapters 5-6)

### Rule D72: Results Equation
Market Understanding × (Self-Understanding × Strategy) = Results
- Market understanding alone is insufficient. Self-understanding is the pivotal element.
- At least 75% of results come from self-understanding once market knowledge is solid.

### Rule D73: Whole-Brained Trading
- **Left brain** (analytical): Preparation, homework, post-market analysis, rule formulation
- **Right brain** (intuitive): During live trading, pattern recognition, gut feelings
- Success requires BOTH — neither alone is sufficient
- Pre-market = left brain dominant. During market = right brain dominant.

### Rule D74: Trading Journal
Keep a trading journal documenting thoughts, feelings, and circumstances before/during/after each trade. Identify behavioral patterns that affect performance.

### Rule D75: Trading Is a Business
Twelve strategic elements:
1. Capital (adequate capitalization)
2. Location (good trade location — always)
3. Timing (patience; wait for excess before acting)
4. Information (reliable, objective; eliminate conflicting sources)
5. Know your competition (assess inventory — too long? too short?)
6. Know yourself (strengths, weaknesses, biases)
7. Consistent daily execution (cover overhead daily)
8. Inventory management (build positions at extremes, not middle)
9. Risk (play short odds; limit maximum trade exposure)
10. Goals (clear, measurable, reviewed regularly)
11. Record keeping (objective performance evaluation)
12. Dedication (the intangible driving force)

## XV. CRITICAL IMPLEMENTATION NOTES

### What Dalton provides vs. what we must calibrate:
1. **Dalton is CONCEPTUAL, not numeric.** IB "wide" vs "narrow" has no pixel count. We must calibrate.
2. **Day types require Market Profile infrastructure** — TPO charts, not just bar charts. Our system uses bar data. We can APPROXIMATE day types from OHLCV data but not perfectly.
3. **Value Area requires TPO or volume-at-price data.** Approximated from OHLCV by treating the middle 70% of range as VA (rough proxy).
4. **POC requires volume-at-price or TPO counting.** Can proxy with VWAP or mid-range of high-volume area.
5. **One-timeframing detection is straightforward** from intraday data but requires intraday bars (not just daily).
6. **The framework is most powerful at the daily timeframe** — comparing today's open/VA/range to yesterday's.

### What IS directly implementable from daily OHLCV:
- Day type classification (approximate from range, IB proxy, close location)
- Open vs previous day's range (within VA, within range, outside range)
- Value Area placement sequences (higher/lower/overlapping)
- Directional performance (volume × VA placement combinations)
- Trend vs bracket identification (VA overlap patterns)
- Gap analysis (all three gap types)
- P-formation / b-formation detection (close location + range shape)
- 3-to-I day detection (tail + TPO imbalance + range extension alignment)
- Poor high/low detection (no tail at extremes)
- Spike detection (late-session range extension)
