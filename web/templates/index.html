Method II · Chapter 16
#1 ★★★★★ Best Accuracy — 63.4% SELL accuracy, strongest trend confirmation with %b + MFI dual validation
Trend Following — %b + MFI Confirmation
Bollinger's "trust but verify" approach. %b tells you where price sits within the bands (0 = lower band, 1 = upper band). MFI (Money Flow Index) tells you if real money is behind the move. When both agree, the trend is genuine. When they diverge — price is high but money flow is weak — the trend is a lie about to fail. This is how you separate real breakouts from fake rallies.
"Use %b to clarify trading patterns generated by price action. Use indicators to confirm. %b tells you where you are within the bands; MFI confirms if there is enough money flowing in to sustain a move. The combination is remarkably powerful." — John Bollinger, Chapter 16: Method II
Understanding %b
%b = 1.0 → price is exactly AT the upper band. %b = 0.0 → price is at the lower band. %b > 1.0 → price is ABOVE upper band (strong breakout). %b < 0.0 → price is BELOW lower band (extreme weakness). %b = 0.5 → price is at the middle band (neutral zone).
%b > 0.8 = bullish zone %b < 0.2 = bearish zone MFI > 80 = strong inflow MFI < 20 = weak outflow Divergence = danger
How it Works — Step by Step
▲ BUY — Confirmed Uptrend:
  1. %b > 0.8 — price is in the upper 20% of the bands (strong position)
  2. MFI > 80 — money is actively flowing into the stock (institutional support)
  3. Both rising together — %b trending up AND MFI trending up = confirmed trend
▼ SELL — Confirmed Downtrend:
  1. %b < 0.2 — price is in the lower 20% of bands (weak position)
  2. MFI < 20 — money is flowing OUT of the stock (distribution)
  3. Both falling together — %b down AND MFI down = confirmed downtrend
⚠ DIVERGENCE — Trend Exhaustion Warning:
  1. %b HIGH but MFI LOW — price is rising but money isn't behind it → rally will fail
  2. %b LOW but MFI HIGH — price is falling but money is flowing in → selloff may reverse
Pro Tip from the Book
Bollinger says this method works best when combined with Method I. After a squeeze breakout, use %b + MFI to confirm whether the breakout has real money behind it or is a head fake. Also watch for volume confirmation — moves on above-average volume are far more reliable.
Common Mistake
Don't sell just because %b is high. A %b of 0.95 doesn't mean "overbought" — it means the stock is strong. Only sell when MFI diverges (MFI falling while %b stays high). "High %b with high MFI is bullish, not bearish."

Method I · Chapters 15–16
#2 ★★★★☆ Most Signals — 57.5% SELL accuracy, generates the most signals (2,600+ in backtest), cornerstone of the system
Volatility Breakout — The Squeeze
The squeeze play is the cornerstone of Bollinger's system. When Bollinger Bands narrow to their tightest point (BandWidth at a 6-month low), volatility has compressed like a loaded spring. Bollinger's central thesis: low volatility begets high volatility — the tighter the squeeze, the more explosive the breakout. The stock will make a big move; your job is to determine the direction.
"Periods of low volatility are often followed by periods of high volatility. When the bands narrow, a sharp price move in either direction is likely. This is the single most useful feature of Bollinger Bands." — John Bollinger, Chapter 15: The Squeeze
Core Concept — Volatility Cycle
Markets alternate between contraction (quiet, range-bound) and expansion (trending, volatile). The squeeze catches the exact transition point. BandWidth = (Upper − Lower) / Middle. When it hits a 6-month low, the spring is fully loaded.
BandWidth at 6mo Low %b > 1.0 = breakout CMF > 0 = buying pressure MFI > 50 = strong flow Volume Surge
How it Works — Step by Step
▲ BUY SIGNAL — All 5 conditions must be TRUE:
  1. Squeeze active — BandWidth at 6-month low (bands are the tightest they've been)
  2. Breakout confirmed — Price closes above the upper Bollinger Band (%b > 1.0)
  3. Money flowing IN — CMF > 0 (Chaikin Money Flow positive = institutional buying)
  4. Volume-weighted confirmation — MFI > 50 (Money Flow Index shows money entering the stock)
  5. Volume surge — Today's volume > 50-day average (smart money is active)
▼ SELL / EXIT — any of these triggers:
  1. SAR Flip — Parabolic SAR flips above price (trend reversal signal)
  2. Lower band tag — Price touches/crosses the lower Bollinger Band
  3. Double negative — CMF < 0 AND MFI < 50 simultaneously (money leaving)
Common Mistake — The Head Fake
Bollinger warns that head fakes are common: price breaks above the band but immediately reverses. Always confirm with CMF + MFI + Volume. If the breakout happens on declining volume, it's likely a fake-out. "The head fake is actually the most common outcome of a squeeze."
Pro Tip
Count the squeeze days — the longer the squeeze persists, the more powerful the eventual breakout. A squeeze lasting 15+ days often produces multi-week moves. Use the direction lean (Bullish/Bearish/Neutral) to anticipate which way.

Method III · Chapter 17
#3 ★★★☆☆ High Precision, Rare — 75% confidence when found, detects W-Bottoms & M-Tops but strict pattern rules mean fewer signals
Reversals — W-Bottoms & M-Tops
The most powerful pattern-based strategy in Bollinger's toolbox. Built on Arthur Merrill's research into 16 variations of W and M patterns. The breakthrough insight: use %b to define the pattern, not price alone. A W-Bottom (double bottom) where the second low has higher %b than the first is a classic buy signal. An M-Top (double top) where the second high has lower %b is a sell signal. The key rule from the book: "The first touch can be outside the band, but the second touch MUST be inside."
"W bottoms were identified by Arthur Merrill. Bollinger Bands provide an ideal tool for identifying them. A W bottom is made when a reaction low is formed near or below the lower band. The stock then pulls back toward the middle band and makes a new price low. Crucially, this new low holds above the lower band. The relative strength shown by the second low being above the band is the signal." — John Bollinger, Chapter 17: Method III — Reversals
The Golden Rule of W-Bottoms & M-Tops
W-Bottom (BUY): 1st low has %b ≤ 0 (at or below lower band) → bounce → 2nd low has %b > 0.2 (ABOVE lower band). The price is the same or lower, but %b is HIGHER = strength building.
M-Top (SELL): 1st high has %b ≥ 1.0 (at or above upper band) → pullback → 2nd high has %b < 0.8 (BELOW upper band). Price is similar, but %b is LOWER = momentum fading.
W-Bottom = BUY M-Top = SELL %b Divergence MFI Confirms 5–25 day spacing
How it Works — Step by Step
▲ BUY — W-Bottom Pattern (Double Bottom Reversal):
  1. First low tags/breaks lower band — %b ≤ 0 (price hits bottom of bands, extreme weakness)
  2. Bounce toward middle band — price recovers, showing some buying interest
  3. Second low STAYS ABOVE lower band — %b > 0.2 (this is the KEY: strength is building underneath)
  4. MFI divergence confirms — MFI is higher at the 2nd low than the 1st (money flowing in despite price retest)
  5. BUY on the break above the middle band — pattern confirmed, enter the trade
▼ SELL — M-Top Pattern (Double Top Reversal):
  1. First high tags/breaks upper band — %b ≥ 1.0 (price is at the top, euphoria)
  2. Pullback toward middle band — profit-taking begins
  3. Second high FAILS to reach upper band — %b < 0.8 (this is the KEY: momentum is dying)
  4. MFI divergence confirms — MFI is lower at 2nd high (smart money leaving despite price rally)
  5. SELL on the break below the middle band — distribution confirmed, exit the trade
"M tops are more complex and dangerous. The key diagnostic element is that the second high is lower than the first on a %b basis even if it is higher in price. This is a failure of momentum — the most important technical diagnostic tool available." — John Bollinger, Chapter 17: M-Tops
Pro Tip — Merrill's 16 Variations
Not all W-Bottoms look the same. Arthur Merrill catalogued 16 different W-shapes. The scanner looks for the most common ones where the 2nd low is within 3% of the 1st low in price, but with significantly higher %b. Spacing between lows should be 5–25 trading days apart.
Common Mistake
Don't buy at the first low just because price hit the lower band. Bollinger says: "The first touch of the lower band in a W bottom is a reaction low. Wait for the second test." The confirmation comes from the 2nd low holding above the band.

Method IV · Chapter 18
#4 ★★☆☆☆ Trend Management — Best used as a holding tool after entry, confirms existing trends rather than generating new entries
Walking the Bands — Trend Confirmation
This is the strategy that separates amateurs from professionals. Most traders see price hitting the upper band and think "overbought — sell!". Bollinger says that's exactly wrong. In strong trends, price walks along the upper band, repeatedly tagging it. Each tag is NOT a sell signal — it's a confirmation that the trend is alive. Similarly, in a downtrend, price walks the lower band — each touch confirms the selling pressure. The walk ends only when price pulls back to the middle band and fails to resume.
"Tags of the bands are just that — tags, not signals. A tag of the upper Bollinger Band is NOT in and of itself a sell signal. A tag of the lower Bollinger Band is NOT in and of itself a buy signal. Price can and does 'walk the band.' In strong uptrends, closes repeatedly at or near the upper band are a sign of strength, not weakness." — John Bollinger, Chapter 18: Walking the Bands
The Counter-Intuitive Truth
Upper band walk = BULLISH — price tagging the upper band 3+ times in 10 days means institutional buying is relentless. DO NOT SELL.
Lower band walk = BEARISH — price tagging the lower band 3+ times in 10 days means selling pressure is overwhelming. DO NOT BUY.
Exit signal — only when price pulls back to the middle band (20-day SMA) and fails to bounce back to the band it was walking.
Upper Walk = Stay Long Lower Walk = Stay Short ≥ 3 touches in 10 bars %b > 0.85 sustained %b < 0.15 sustained Mid-band = exit zone
How it Works — Step by Step
▲ BUY — Upper Band Walk (Strong Uptrend):
  1. Price tags upper band ≥ 3 times in the last 10 trading days (within 0.5% of band = "touch")
  2. %b stays above 0.85 consistently — price is camped in the upper zone of the bands
  3. Pullbacks stop at the middle band — the 20-day SMA acts as support; buyers step in on every dip
  4. Stay long / buy dips to middle band — each touch of the upper band CONFIRMS strength
▼ SELL — Lower Band Walk (Strong Downtrend):
  1. Price tags lower band ≥ 3 times in the last 10 trading days (persistent selling)
  2. %b stays below 0.15 consistently — price is pinned to the bottom of the bands
  3. Bounces fail at the middle band — 20-day SMA now acts as resistance; sellers return on every rally
  4. Stay short / sell rallies to middle band — do NOT buy thinking "it's cheap"
"In a strong uptrend, the close will repeatedly be at or near the upper band. In fact, dips to the middle band provide buying opportunities. The inverse is true for a downtrend. The most common mistake in applying Bollinger Bands is selling at the upper band and buying at the lower band." — John Bollinger, Chapter 18
The #1 Bollinger Band Mistake
Selling at the upper band or buying at the lower band mechanically is WRONG. Bollinger wrote an entire chapter to combat this myth. The bands are NOT overbought/oversold lines. A stock touching the upper band in a walk is showing strength, and a stock touching the lower band in a walk is showing weakness. "The bands are NOT targets. They are indications of trend strength."
Pro Tip — Combining Methods
After a Method I squeeze breakout, the stock often enters a Method IV upper band walk. This combination is extremely powerful — the squeeze provides the entry, and the walk tells you to hold on and let profits run. Exit only when the walk ends (price fails to bounce off the middle band) or when Method II divergence appears (MFI falling while %b stays high).
