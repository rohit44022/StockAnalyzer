📊 Signal Scoring Breakdown
📖 What is this? Technical analysis uses dozens of indicators, but no single indicator is perfect. John Murphy (Ch 19) recommends combining multiple tools for CONFIRMATION — like a doctor getting multiple test results before a diagnosis. Here, we combine 6 categories with different weights: Trend (25%) tells you the direction, Momentum (20%) tells you the speed, Volume (15%) confirms if real money is behind the move, Patterns (15%) shows chart formations, Support/Resistance (10%) shows price floors/ceilings, and Risk (15%) assesses danger levels. A positive score in a category means bullish evidence; negative means bearish.
🎯 Target Prices & Price Projections
📖 What are Target Prices? A target price is an estimated future price where a stock may move based on technical analysis. Unlike fundamental analysis (which values a company based on earnings), technical targets are based on chart mathematics — past price swings, support/resistance zones, and statistical volatility. We use 7 independent methods and combine them into a confidence-weighted consensus. Important: Target prices are projections, not guarantees. They help you set realistic profit expectations and plan your exit strategy.

📈 Moving Averages
📖 What are Moving Averages? A Moving Average (MA) smooths out price data by calculating the average price over a specific period. Think of it like this: if a stock's price jumps around daily between ₹95-₹105, the 20-day average might be ₹100 — showing the TRUE direction ignoring daily noise.

Key MAs to know:
• SMA 20 — Short-term trend (used by swing traders). Acts as immediate support/resistance.
• SMA 50 — Medium-term trend. When price is above this, the medium-term outlook is positive.
• SMA 200 — Long-term trend. This is THE most important moving average. Institutional investors use it to decide major allocation. Below 200-SMA = bear market territory.
• Golden Cross — When 50-SMA crosses ABOVE 200-SMA → Strong Buy signal. Death Cross — opposite → Strong Sell.
• EMA (Exponential) reacts faster than SMA because it gives more weight to recent prices. Traders use EMA 9/21 for quick signals.
🔄 Oscillators & Momentum
📖 What are Oscillators? Oscillators measure the SPEED and STRENGTH of price movements. They swing between fixed boundaries (like 0-100) and help answer: "Has the stock moved too far too fast?"

Key Oscillators explained:
• RSI (Relative Strength Index, 0-100) — Measures buying vs selling pressure. Above 70 = OVERBOUGHT (price rose too fast, pullback likely). Below 30 = OVERSOLD (price fell too much, bounce likely). Think of it as a "tiredness meter" for the stock.
• MACD — Shows if short-term momentum is speeding up or slowing down. When MACD line crosses above signal line = Buy. Histogram bars growing = momentum increasing.
• Stochastic %K/%D (0-100) — Compares closing price to its price range. Above 80 = overbought, below 20 = oversold. %K crossing above %D = Buy signal.
• Williams %R (-100 to 0) — Similar to Stochastic but inverted. Above -20 = overbought, below -80 = oversold.
• CCI (Commodity Channel Index) — Measures how far price has deviated from its average. Above +100 = extended bullish, below -100 = extended bearish.
• ADX (Average Directional Index, 0-100) — Does NOT tell direction, only STRENGTH. Above 25 = strong trend, below 20 = weak/no trend. Use +DI/-DI for direction.
📊 Volume Analysis
📖 Why Volume Matters: Volume is the number of shares traded — it reveals the CONVICTION behind a price move. A stock rising on high volume = genuine buying (big players are committing money). A stock rising on low volume = suspicious (nobody cares, likely to reverse).

• Volume Ratio — Current volume ÷ 20-day average. Above 1.5x = high participation. Below 0.5x = very low interest.
• OBV (On-Balance Volume) — Running total of volume: adds on up-days, subtracts on down-days. If OBV is rising while price is flat → smart money is quietly accumulating (bullish!). If OBV is falling while price is rising → distribution happening (bearish warning!).
• VWAP (Volume Weighted Average Price) — The average price weighted by volume. Institutional traders use VWAP as a benchmark. Price above VWAP = institutions are buying; below = they're selling.
🕯️ Candlestick Patterns
📖 What are Candlestick Patterns? Each "candle" on a chart shows 4 prices: Open, High, Low, Close. The body shape and shadow/wick lengths reveal the battle between buyers and sellers WITHIN that day.

Key patterns to know:
• Hammer/Inverted Hammer — Found at bottoms. Long lower wick shows sellers tried to push price down but buyers fought back hard. Bullish reversal signal.
• Engulfing — Today's candle completely "swallows" yesterday's. Bullish engulfing at a bottom = strong reversal. Bearish engulfing at a top = warning.
• Doji — Open ≈ Close (tiny body). Shows indecision — neither buyers nor sellers won. After a strong trend, a doji warns of potential reversal.
• Morning Star — 3-candle pattern: big red candle → small candle (indecision) → big green candle. Strong bullish reversal at bottoms.
• Shooting Star — Small body at bottom, long upper wick. Sellers rejected higher prices. Bearish at tops.
⚠️ Important: Candlestick patterns are most reliable at key support/resistance levels and when confirmed by volume.
🔺 Chart Patterns
📖 What are Chart Patterns? Chart patterns are geometric shapes that form on price charts over days/weeks. They represent the psychological battle between buyers and sellers and can predict the next big move.

Two types:
• Reversal Patterns — Signal that the current trend is about to change direction:
  - Head & Shoulders (bearish reversal), Inverse H&S (bullish reversal)
  - Double Top (M-shape, bearish), Double Bottom (W-shape, bullish)
  - Rounding Top/Bottom
• Continuation Patterns — Signal that the current trend will continue after a pause:
  - Triangles (ascending = bullish, descending = bearish, symmetric = breakout either way)
  - Flags and Pennants (brief consolidation in a strong trend)
  - Wedges (rising wedge = bearish, falling wedge = bullish)
📐 Measured Move: Most patterns have a "target" calculated by measuring the pattern's height and projecting from the breakout point.
📖 Support & Resistance Explained: Support is a price FLOOR where buyers consistently step in to buy. Resistance is a price CEILING where sellers consistently appear. The more times a level is tested, the stronger it becomes. Key insight: When support BREAKS, it becomes the new resistance (and vice versa) — this is one of the most powerful concepts in technical analysis. Fibonacci levels (38.2%, 50%, 61.8%) are mathematical ratios derived from the golden ratio — markets tend to retrace to these levels before continuing the trend.

☁️ Ichimoku Cloud Analysis
📖 What is Ichimoku Cloud? Ichimoku Kinko Hyo (Japanese: "one glance equilibrium chart") is the ONLY indicator that gives you trend direction, support/resistance, and momentum ALL in one look. It has 5 lines:
• Tenkan-sen (Conversion Line) — 9-period midpoint. Like a fast moving average. Shows short-term momentum.
• Kijun-sen (Base Line) — 26-period midpoint. The key support/resistance line. Flat Kijun = price will pull back to it.
• Senkou Span A & B (Cloud) — The area between these two lines forms the "cloud." Price ABOVE the cloud = bullish. Price BELOW = bearish. Price INSIDE = no trend. A thicker cloud = stronger support/resistance.
• Chikou Span (Lagging) — Current closing price plotted 26 periods back. If it's above the historical price, the trend is confirmed.

🛡️ Risk Management
📖 Why Risk Management is #1: John Murphy (Ch 16) states that money management is MORE important than the entry signal. Even the best strategy will fail without proper risk control.

Key rules every trader must follow:
• 2% Rule — NEVER risk more than 2% of your total capital on a single trade. If you have ₹5,00,000, your maximum risk per trade is ₹10,000.
• Stop Loss is MANDATORY — Before you buy, ALWAYS know where you'll sell if wrong. Common methods: 2× ATR below entry, below nearest support, or below a key moving average.
• Risk:Reward minimum 1:2 — Only enter trades where potential profit is at least 2× the potential loss. This means even if you're right only 40% of the time, you'll still be profitable.
• Position Sizing — The number of shares you buy should be calculated from your stop loss distance, NOT from how much you want to invest.
