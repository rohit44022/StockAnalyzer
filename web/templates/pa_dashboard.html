About This Engine
This system implements Al Brooks' price action methodology from "Trading Price Action: TRENDS". It reads raw price bars (candlesticks) without relying on lagging indicators like RSI or MACD. Instead, it analyzes what buyers and sellers are actually doing — bar by bar — to determine who is in control and where the next high-probability trade setup is forming.

What Makes This Different? Most trading systems use indicator crossovers (moving averages, RSI overbought/oversold). Price action reads the story behind each bar — the struggle between buyers and sellers, momentum shifts, failed breakouts, and trapped traders. This gives earlier, more reliable signals because you're reading the cause (price), not the effect (indicators).

Core Concepts

Price Action means reading the raw price chart — just the bars (candlesticks) — without relying on indicators like RSI or MACD. Think of it like reading body language: the price bars "tell a story" about what buyers and sellers are doing. Every bar shows who's winning (buyers vs sellers) and how hard they're fighting. Al Brooks' entire method is based on reading this story bar by bar.

In simple terms: Instead of looking at fancy lines and colors from indicators, you look at the actual price movement — where it opened, where it closed, how high and low it went. That's price action.

Always-In Direction is Al Brooks' most important concept. Imagine you MUST be in a trade at all times — either long (betting price goes up) or short (betting price goes down). Which would you choose right now? That's the always-in direction.

LONG = The overall pressure is UP. If forced to pick, you'd be buying.
SHORT = The overall pressure is DOWN. If forced to pick, you'd be selling.
FLAT = It's unclear — no strong direction. Best to wait.

Why it matters: You should only take BUY trades when always-in is LONG, and SELL trades when always-in is SHORT. Trading against the always-in direction is like swimming against the current.

A Signal Bar is the bar that "signals" you to prepare for a trade. It tells you: "Hey, something is about to happen." For a BUY signal, this is often a strong bull bar (closes near its high). For a SELL signal, it's a strong bear bar (closes near its low).

The Entry Bar is the very next bar after the signal bar — this is where you actually enter the trade. You place your order when the entry bar goes above (for BUY) or below (for SELL) the signal bar.

Think of it like traffic lights: The signal bar is the yellow light (get ready), and the entry bar is the green light (go!).

Bull Trend Bar — A strong bar where the close is much higher than the open. The body (colored part) is at least 60% of the total bar. This means buyers are winning convincingly.

Bear Trend Bar — The opposite. Close is much lower than the open. Sellers are dominating.

Doji — A bar where open and close are nearly equal (very small body). It means neither buyers nor sellers won that round — a draw. Dojis often appear at turning points.

Shaved Bar — A bar with no tail (shadow/wick) on one end. A "shaved top" bull bar closed at its absolute highest price — extreme buyer conviction. Same idea for shaved bottom bear bars.
Bar Patterns

An Inside Bar is a bar whose high is lower than the previous bar's high AND whose low is higher than the previous bar's low. It's completely "inside" the previous bar's range. This means the market is taking a breather — pausing.

ii = Two consecutive inside bars. Like two pauses in a row — extra compression.
iii = Three inside bars. Very rare and very powerful — the spring is coiled tight.
ioi = Inside bar, then an outside bar (bigger), then another inside bar. A complex squeeze pattern.

Why it matters: Inside bars = coiled spring. When the market finally breaks out of this tight range, the move is often fast and strong. The more inside bars in a row, the bigger the expected breakout.

An Outside Bar is the opposite of an inside bar — its high is ABOVE the previous bar's high AND its low is BELOW the previous bar's low. It completely "swallows" the previous bar.

This means both buyers and sellers made strong moves within that bar. It often signals a potential reversal or the start of a big move. The direction it closes in (up or down) often hints at which side won.

In a pullback (a temporary dip in an uptrend or rise in a downtrend), Al Brooks counts how many times price tries to resume:

H1, H2, H3, H4 — In a bull trend, when price pulls back, the first bar that goes above the previous bar's high is called H1 (High 1). If it fails and pulls back again, the next attempt is H2. Then H3, H4.

L1, L2, L3, L4 — Same idea in a bear trend. L1 is the first bar that goes below the previous bar's low during a pullback.

The golden rule: H2 (or L2) is the "second entry" — Al Brooks considers this the highest-probability trade because it means the trend tried to resume, failed once, and is now trying again with more conviction. Most professional traders wait for the H2/L2 entry.

H4/L4 = The trend may be exhausted. Four attempts to resume suggests the pullback might become a reversal.

Wedge — Three pushes in the same direction. In an uptrend: three higher highs, each weaker than the last (like a tired runner). In a downtrend: three lower lows. After the third push, the trend often reverses. Think of it as "three strikes and you're out."

Double Top (DT / M-Top) — Price reaches a high, pulls back, then reaches approximately the same high again but can't go higher. It looks like the letter "M." This often means buyers are exhausted — expect a drop.

Double Bottom (DB / W-Bottom) — The mirror image. Price hits a low, bounces, hits about the same low again, then bounces harder. Looks like "W." Sellers are exhausted — expect a rise.

Bear Flag / Bull Flag — A brief pause (flag) in the middle of a trend. A bull flag is a small pullback in an uptrend — it's just the trend catching its breath before continuing up. Bear flag = small bounce in a downtrend before continuing down.
Trend Concepts

Most trends have two phases:

Spike — The explosive start. Price moves rapidly with big trend bars and little or no overlap between bars. This is like a sprinter's start — fast and powerful. Spikes are driven by urgency (everyone rushing to buy or sell at the same time).

Channel — After the spike, the trend continues but in a calmer, more orderly way. Price makes steady progress with small pullbacks along the way. Think of it like a jogger after a sprint — still moving forward, but at a sustainable pace.

Trading tip: The best entries happen at the START of a spike or on pullbacks within a channel. Entering at the END of a channel is risky because the trend is aging.

A Micro Channel is a very tight channel where almost every bar moves in the trend direction with very tiny pullbacks (or none at all). For a bull micro channel, every bar's low is at or above the previous bar's low.

This is extremely bullish (or bearish for a bear micro channel) — it means the trend is so strong that the other side can't even create a one-bar pullback.

Caution: While micro channels show extreme strength, they're also unsustainable. When they finally break, the pullback can be sharp. Don't chase the end of a micro channel.

Two-Leg Pullback — Al Brooks says "the market does everything in twos." When a trend pulls back, it usually does so in two distinct legs (two separate down-swings in a bull trend). After the second leg completes, the trend usually resumes.

Measured Move — The second leg of a move often equals the first leg in size. So if the first leg went up ₹50, the second leg after a pullback will likely also go up about ₹50. This gives you a price target.

How to use it: Wait for two legs of pullback, then enter. Set your target as a measured move from the pullback low. This is one of the most reliable patterns in all of trading.

Buying Pressure = How aggressively buyers are acting. Measured by: bull bars closing near their highs, bars closing above the previous bar's close, lower tails being rejected (buyers stepping in at lows).

Selling Pressure = The opposite — bear bars closing near lows, closes below previous close, upper tails being rejected (sellers stepping in at highs).

This system measures both on a scale. When buying pressure strongly exceeds selling pressure, the always-in direction tilts LONG. The reverse for SHORT.

Analogy: Think of a tug-of-war. Buying pressure is one team pulling left, selling pressure is the other team pulling right. Whichever side is pulling harder determines the trend direction.
Setups & Entry Types

Breakout — When price moves beyond a key level (a previous high, low, or trendline). It's like water breaking through a dam. A strong breakout with big bars and high volume often leads to a sustained trend.

Failed Breakout — Price breaks a level but quickly reverses back. This is one of the MOST reliable trading signals. Why? Because everyone who bought the breakout is now trapped — they'll need to sell, which pushes price further in the opposite direction.

Example: Stock hits ₹500 resistance, breaks to ₹510, but then drops back to ₹495. Everyone who bought at ₹505-510 is now losing money and panicking. Their selling accelerates the down move. A failed breakout above becomes a sell signal, and vice versa.

The Second Entry is Al Brooks' bread-and-butter trade. In a bull trend pullback, H1 (first attempt to resume) often fails. H2 (the second attempt) has a much higher success rate — typically 60%+ probability.

Why? The H1 failure shakes out weak longs and attracts shorts. When H2 forms, both the longs re-entering AND the shorts covering their losing positions push price up. It's a double engine of buying power.

When to use: In a clear bull trend, wait for a pullback. The first high (H1) forms. If it fails and pulls back again, watch for H2. Enter above H2's high with a stop below the pullback low. This is the highest-probability with-trend entry.

Pullback — A temporary move against the trend. In an uptrend, a pullback is a small dip. It's normal and healthy — trends need to pause and "reload" before continuing.

Trend Continuation — After the pullback completes, the trend resumes its original direction. This is the safest type of trade — you're joining an existing trend rather than trying to predict a reversal.

How deep is too deep? If a pullback retraces more than 50% of the previous move, it might not be a pullback — it could be the start of a reversal. Shallow pullbacks (25-38% retracement) are strongest.

Reversal — When the trend changes direction. A bull trend becomes a bear trend, or vice versa. Reversals usually start with a climax or exhaustion pattern.

Climax — An extreme move that exhausts one side. A buy climax is a series of big bull bars at the top of a trend — the last burst of enthusiasm before sellers take over. "The hottest flame burns out fastest."

Signs of a reversal: Multiple climax bars followed by a strong reversal bar, a failed breakout at a key level, and 3 pushes (wedge) completing. Reversals are harder to trade than trend continuations — only advanced traders should attempt them.
Trading Ranges & Phases

A Trading Range is when price goes sideways — bouncing between a ceiling (resistance) and a floor (support) without making progress in either direction. Al Brooks says the market spends about 80% of its time in trading ranges.

Key insight: In a trading range, most breakout attempts fail. Buy low (near support), sell high (near resistance), and wait for clear signs of a real breakout before committing to a trend trade.

Tight Channel — Price is moving in a narrow range with small pullbacks. The trend lines are close together. Very strong trend — don't bet against it.

Broad Channel — Price is trending but with wide swings back and forth. Each pullback is significant. This is easier for swing traders — buy at the bottom of the channel, sell at the top.

Phases of a trend (Al Brooks model):
1. Spike → 2. Tight Channel → 3. Broad Channel → 4. Trading Range
As a trend ages, channels tend to widen. A transition from tight to broad channel is an early warning that the trend may be weakening.
Risk Management

Stop Loss — The price at which you exit a losing trade. Al Brooks typically places stops one tick beyond the signal bar's extreme. For a BUY: stop goes one tick below the signal bar's low. For a SELL: one tick above the signal bar's high.

Position Sizing — How many shares to buy. The golden rule: never risk more than 1-2% of your capital on a single trade. If your capital is ₹5,00,000 and your risk per trade is 1%, you can risk ₹5,000 maximum. If the stop loss is ₹10 away from your entry, you can buy 500 shares (₹5,000 ÷ ₹10).

Risk:Reward Ratio — How much you can gain vs how much you might lose. A 1:2 ratio means for every ₹1 risked, you could gain ₹2. Al Brooks recommends at least 1:2 for most trades and 1:1 only for very high-probability setups.

Follow-Through means what happens AFTER a signal. If a strong bull signal bar forms and the next bar (entry bar) also closes higher — that's good follow-through. It confirms the signal.

If the entry bar is a doji or a bear bar instead — that's poor follow-through. The signal may be failing.

Rule of thumb: Wait for one bar of follow-through before trusting a signal. A strong signal with no follow-through is like thunder with no rain — it might be a false alarm.
